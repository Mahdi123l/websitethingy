Google have a service called Street View which allows a user to view surroundings from street-level. Google has extended their Street View service to cover the inside of buildings such as museums and sports stadiums. This raises several ethical, legal, and cultural considerations.

To start, Street View would be capturing indoor spaces, which could easily infringe upon people's privacy, as people may not expect to be recorded in said environments, Google would need to obtain consent from any individuals present in the indoor spaces before they would be able to capture and publish the images.

Street View also poses a risk to security by covering the inside of buildings. Mapping of some buildings may reveal confidential information about a buildings layout, making it easier for unauthorised individuals to target those buildings

Legal issues such as Copyright could also become a serious issue for Google's Street View, as in places such as Meuseums, artworks may be copyrighted by the Meuseum, meaning Google would need to ask for permission in order to display these artworks in digital form. Meanwhile commercial venues such as sports stadiums may have legal rights over images taken inside them, meaning Google would need to ask for permission to use those too.

Further legal issues follow, as Google must make sure that these images accurately depict the environment within buildings in order to avoid misinterpretation and potential legal claims - if users rely on the indoor mapping for navigation, inaccuracies could lead to accidents and subsequent liability concerns.

Culturally sensitive areas would demand respect for the heritage and traditions of said cultures, images of the inside of these areas would need to consider the beliefs and customs associated with the spaces; The representation of the areas may not fully capture the cultural significance of these areas, potentially leading to misinterpretation, the overexposure to the representation could potentially impact the uniqueness and mystery surrounding certain cultural or historical areas. In conclusion, Google extending its Street View to include some indoor spaces creates a wide range of ethical, legal, and cultural concerns. Overall, balancing the need for public access to information with privacy, security, and cultural sensitivities is essential as technology continues to shape how we experience physical places.

Between 2008 and 2010, a company that was gathering data for an online mapping system, using cars fitted with cameras and WiFi equipment, collected some information that was being transmitted on personal WiFi networks. The company apologised for doing this and an investigation found that a small number of software developers had been responsible for adding this functionality to the mapping system data collection software.

This scenario brings forth several points for discussion: the method of unintended data collection, potential preventive steps, legal and ethical issues and the lessons learned by the company from this experience.

The process of data collection in this context is notable. The cars, equipped with cameras and WiFi equipment, were intended to collect mapping data. Yet, they unintentionally intercepted private WiFi transmissions. The cause of this was the inclusion of data collection in the code by the software developers that worked on the mapping software.

Before this event could have occured, WiFi network owners could have taken several different precautions. Encryption, which protects data by encoding it, could have acted as a significant deterrent, as this way the data would not make sense to anyone collecting it. Similarly, hiding network identifiers (SSIDs) could have limited this event.

The legal and ethical considerations stemming from this incident are significant. The data interception breaches upon personal privacy, possibly infringing established privacy laws. This occurrence could include unauthorized eavesdropping on private conversations. From an ethical standpoint, this incident resulted in harm to trust and the importance of safeguarding individual data and maintaining digital privacy.

This incident offers several important lessons. Rigorous oversight over software development processes is vital to prevent the inclusion of undisclosed features. This occurrence highlights the necessity for user consent mechanisms, demanding improvements for user agreement before data collection. Simultaneously, enhancing data security through encryption emerges as a critical preventative measure. The incident further emphasises the need for periodic security assessments to strengthen defenses against potential breaches.

In conclusion, this incident unfolds many issues regarding technology, legality, and ethics. These aspects underscore the importance of data handling, transparent communication, and the preservation of individual privacy within the digital landscape.
